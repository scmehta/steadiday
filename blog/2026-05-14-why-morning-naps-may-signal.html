Why Morning Naps May Signal Higher Mortality Risk

May 14, 2026 • By SteadiDay Team • 7 min read

Most of us have been told that a good nap is practically a health strategy. And for years, the science seemed to back that up — a short rest in the early afternoon, the thinking went, could sharpen focus and ease fatigue. But a major new study published in JAMA Network Open in 2026 is complicating that picture in ways worth paying attention to. Researchers found that daytime napping and mortality risk in older adults are meaningfully connected — and that the details of how you nap matter as much as whether you nap at all.

What the New Research Actually Found

The study, led by researchers from Mass General Brigham and Rush University Medical Center, followed 1,338 adults aged 56 and older for up to 19 years. What made it stand out wasn't just its length — it was its methodology. Rather than asking participants to recall their napping habits, researchers used wrist-worn accelerometers to objectively track sleep and rest patterns over time. That distinction matters. Self-reported sleep data is notoriously unreliable; wearable devices don't forget.

The findings were specific and striking. According to the published research, each additional hour of daytime napping was associated with roughly a 13% higher risk of all-cause mortality. Each extra nap per day added about 7% to that risk. And people who napped primarily in the morning faced a 30% higher mortality risk compared to those who napped in the early afternoon. That morning-nap finding, in particular, is new — and it's the kind of signal that researchers and clinicians hadn't been tracking closely before.

Why Morning Naps Are the Surprising Red Flag

Most people assume a nap is a nap. But timing turns out to be deeply informative. When an older adult naps in the morning — say, between 8 a.m. and noon — it often reflects disrupted nighttime sleep, not simple tiredness from a busy day. The body's natural sleep pressure, called adenosine buildup, shouldn't be strong enough in the morning hours to pull a healthy sleeper into a prolonged rest. Morning napping can be a sign that something disrupted sleep the night before, or that the circadian rhythm itself is shifting in ways that deserve medical attention.

This connects to an important thread in the neurocognitive research. A separate study from the Rush Memory and Aging Project found that more frequent morning naps were linked to higher risk of Alzheimer's dementia, while more regular early-afternoon naps and consistent nap durations were actually associated with lower Alzheimer's-related pathology. In other words, the timing and regularity of napping aren't just lifestyle quirks — they appear to be windows into brain and body health over time.

Neither study is saying that napping causes mortality or dementia. The more accurate read is that certain napping patterns reflect underlying health changes — disrupted circadian rhythms, fragmented nighttime sleep, early neurodegeneration — that are themselves associated with worse outcomes. The nap is the signal, not necessarily the problem.

How Long Is Too Long? What the Numbers Say

Duration is the other piece of this puzzle. The JAMA Network Open findings echo what other large studies have suggested: longer naps carry more risk than short ones. A cohort study of over 6,700 older adults found that midday naps exceeding 60 minutes were associated with increased all-cause mortality, while shorter naps didn't carry the same signal. That same research offered a notable finding on the other side of the equation: meeting physical activity guidelines — at least 150 minutes of moderate-to-vigorous activity per week — appeared to help offset some of the mortality risk tied to abnormal sleep patterns.

That's an actionable number. Not an hour-long midday sleep, but a 20-to-30-minute rest in the early afternoon — the kind that ends before deep sleep stages kick in — is what the evidence generally supports for healthy adults. Crossing into that hour-plus territory, especially in the morning, is where the risk curve starts to bend.

What This Means If You're Over 50

Here's why this research lands differently for adults over 50: sleep architecture genuinely changes with age. You spend less time in deep, restorative slow-wave sleep. Nighttime awakenings become more common. The circadian rhythm can shift earlier, making late nights harder and early mornings unavoidable. All of this makes daytime napping more tempting — and also more informative as a health marker.

If you find yourself consistently napping for more than an hour, napping multiple times a day, or drifting off in the morning hours when you'd rather be active, it's worth bringing up with your doctor. Not because the nap itself is dangerous, but because it may be pointing to something worth investigating — poor sleep quality, a sleep disorder like apnea, cardiovascular changes, or early shifts in cognitive function.

The flip side is reassuring. A single, consistent, reasonably short nap in the early-to-mid afternoon — roughly 1 to 3 p.m. — taken because you feel genuinely tired rather than exhausted, isn't what these studies are flagging. Regularity and moderation appear protective, not harmful.

Practical Steps to Bring Your Nap Habits Into Focus

Knowing the research is one thing. Acting on it is another. Here's what actually helps.

Track your nap timing, not just your nighttime sleep. Most people log when they went to bed and when they woke up, but ignore what happened during the day. Start noticing — even roughly — when you nap, for how long, and what prompted it. A pattern across two weeks tells you far more than a single day.

Protect your nighttime sleep first. Daytime napping often becomes problematic when nighttime sleep is already broken. Consistent wake times, limiting alcohol in the evening, managing light exposure after dark, and keeping the bedroom cool are foundational. If you're sleeping well at night, the urge to nap excessively tends to diminish.

Use your calendar to anchor your rest. This sounds simple, but it works. Scheduling a deliberate 20-minute rest window in the early afternoon — rather than falling asleep whenever fatigue hits — helps keep napping intentional and time-limited. Talk to your doctor if the pattern is shifting. If morning napping is new for you, or if you've noticed your naps getting longer over the past few months, that change is worth mentioning. It's not an emergency — but it's the kind of detail that gets missed if you don't bring it up proactively.

The Bigger Picture on Daytime Napping and Mortality Risk in Older Adults

What makes the 2026 JAMA Network Open study genuinely useful — beyond its size and follow-up period — is that it used real, objective data from wearable devices to document napping behavior as it actually happened, not as people remembered it. That makes its findings harder to dismiss and more directly relevant to the kinds of patterns you might track with a smartwatch or fitness band today.

The takeaway isn't that napping is bad. It's that how you nap is part of your health story. Long, frequent, and morning-timed naps aren't causes for panic — but they are reasons to pay attention. Sleep in later life is one of the clearest windows we have into overall health trajectory, and the evidence is increasingly clear that daytime napping and mortality risk in older adults are linked in ways that are specific, measurable, and — importantly — potentially modifiable.

That last part matters most. These aren't fixed outcomes. They're patterns. And patterns, once you can see them clearly, can be changed.
